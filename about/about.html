About Color Blindness

Definition and Causes

Understanding Color Vision

Color vision is the ability to perceive the different spectrums of light. This is done by light entering the eye and translated via the photoreceptors at the back of the eye. A normal human eye are sensitive to three different wavelengths of light. Hence why humans are known to have a trichomatric color vision.

Colour visions in humans can be split into nine types within four categories. Trichromacy is one of the nine types - those with normal visions. The other eight are known color vision blindness. Color vision blindness are either hereditary, can be caused by disease or injury.

Hereditary Color Blindess

It is possible for color blindness to be passed down from parents to their children. Red-green color blindness is more common in men. This does not mean that women are not susceptible to color blindness. Other categories of color blindness affects both male and female equally.

Color blindness due to disease or injury

Other than hereditary color blindness, a person can also get color blindness from diseases and injuries. Direct injuries to the eye or to the brain may cause a person to get color blindness. Some diseases such as glaucoma and multiple scelorosis that may cause harm to the eye directly or indirectly could also cause color blindness.

Types of Color Vision

Categories

Color vision can be split into four categories:

  • Normal vision
  • red-green blindness
  • blue-yellow blindness
  • complete color blindness

Normal Vision

Trichromacy

A person with Trichromacy is able to see the full spectrum of colors a human eye could see.

Red-green Color Blindness

Protanomaly

A person with Protanomaly sees red colors tinted with more green. It also makes it less bright. This condition is usually mild and does not affect the person's daily life.

Deuternomaly

A person with Deuternomaly sees green colors tinted with more red. This condition is usually mild and does not affect the person's daily life. It is also a common type of red-green color blindness.

Deuternopia

A person with Deuternopia cannot differentiate between red and green.

Protanopia

A person with Protanopia cannot differentiate between red and green.

Blue-Yellow Color Blindness

Tritanomaly

A person with Tritanomaly would find hard to differentiate blue and green, and also yellow and red.

Tritanopia

A person with Tritanopia would find it hard to differentiate blue and green, purple and red, and tellow and pink. They would also see colors less brighter.

Complete/Severe Color Blindness

Achromatomaly

A person with Achromatomaly would find it hard to differentiate any colors.

Achromatopsia

A person with Achromatopsia would not be able to differentiate any colors at all.

Disclaimer

These information are not to be used as self-diagnosis. The information written here are meant as a learning tool and to educate people about color blindness. If you or anyone you know suspects that you may have color-blindness, please reach out to your local medical practitioner for further advice on the matter.
